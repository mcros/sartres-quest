# Background: Sartre's *Nausea* — Plot & Philosophy

Jean-Paul Sartre's "Nausea" (original title: *La Nausée*) is a seminal work of existentialist literature, presented in the form of a diary written by its protagonist, Antoine Roquentin.

## Plot Summary:

The novel follows Antoine Roquentin, a historian living in the fictional French town of Bouville. He is researching the life of an 18th-century political figure, the Marquis de Rollebon, but gradually finds himself gripped by a disturbing and inexplicable sensation he calls "the Nausea." This feeling is not merely physical sickness, but a profound and overwhelming sense of the arbitrariness and superfluity of existence.

As Roquentin's "Nausea" intensifies, he begins to perceive everyday objects and even his own body as foreign, grotesque, and disgustingly present. The solidity and usual meanings of things dissolve, revealing their raw, brute existence. He feels alienated from others, observing them living their lives with a self-assuredness he can no longer comprehend. His research on Rollebon becomes meaningless, as he realizes he can never truly "know" the past or anyone else, and that trying to find meaning in another's life is a futile attempt to escape his own lack of inherent purpose.

Roquentin's journey is one of increasing isolation and despair. He tries to connect with his former lover, Anny, but finds that she too has changed and can offer no solace. He encounters the "Self-Taught Man," a self-proclaimed humanist who believes in the inherent goodness of humanity, but Roquentin finds his optimism naïve and ultimately dismisses it as another form of self-deception.

The climax of his experience occurs in a park, where he has a profound encounter with a chestnut tree root. He realizes that all things, including himself, exist without any inherent reason or justification. They are simply "there," contingent and absurd. This realization is both terrifying and liberating. While it plunges him into deeper despair about the meaninglessness of everything, it also opens up the possibility of creating his own meaning.

The novel ends with Roquentin abandoning his historical research and contemplating the possibility of writing a fictional novel or a piece of music. While he understands this endeavor won't resolve his existential angst, it suggests a potential path for him to create something of value in a world devoid of inherent meaning.

## Philosophical Ideas Explored:

"Nausea" is a literary vehicle for exploring key tenets of Sartre's existentialist philosophy. Here are the main ideas:

- **Existence Precedes Essence:** This is the cornerstone of Sartre's philosophy. For inanimate objects, essence (what it is) precedes existence (that it is). A chair is designed to be a chair before it is created. However, for humans, Sartre argues that we are born into existence without a pre-defined essence or purpose. We simply *are*, and then, through our choices and actions, we define who and what we become. There is no predetermined human nature or divine plan.

- **Contingency and Absurdity:** Roquentin's "Nausea" is the visceral experience of the sheer contingency of existence. Things simply "are," without any underlying reason or necessity. This leads to the feeling of **absurdity** – the inherent lack of meaning in a world that offers no inherent purpose. The world is indifferent to human desires for meaning.

- **Freedom and Responsibility:** Because there is no pre-given essence or meaning, humans are condemned to be free. We are entirely responsible for our choices and actions, and for the meaning we create (or fail to create) in our lives. This radical freedom is often accompanied by **anguish**, as the weight of total responsibility can be terrifying.

- **Bad Faith (Mauvaise Foi):** This concept refers to the act of self-deception, where individuals deny their radical freedom and responsibility by pretending they are not free or by conforming to pre-established roles, values, or excuses. Roquentin observes others in Bouville living in bad faith, adopting roles like "businessman" or "lover" as if these roles define them, rather than acknowledging their freedom to be otherwise.

- **Solipsism and Alienation:** Roquentin's journey emphasizes a profound sense of isolation. His inability to truly connect with others, and his realization that he can only truly know his own subjective experience, highlights the theme of **solipsism**. He feels alienated from both the world and other people, as his newfound awareness of existence sets him apart.

- **The Overwhelming Nature of Existence:** The "Nausea" itself represents the feeling of being overwhelmed by the raw, undifferentiated fact of existence. When the comforting labels and categories we impose on the world dissolve, what's left is a viscous, overflowing, and somewhat repulsive "thingness."

- **The Search for Meaning:** While the novel posits that there is no inherent meaning in the world, it doesn't advocate for nihilism. Instead, it suggests that meaning must be **created** by the individual through their actions, projects, and commitments. Roquentin's contemplation of writing a novel at the end is a tentative step towards creating his own meaning, even if it's a meaning he must constantly re-affirm.
